BAD: Tight line-height on multi-line text

This paragraph has a line-height of 1.0 which means the lines are touching each other. When you have multiple lines of text like this, the descenders of one line crash into the ascenders of the next line. It makes reading extremely uncomfortable and fatiguing, especially for longer passages of text that wrap across multiple lines.

This smaller text also has tight line spacing at 1.1x. The reduced size combined with cramped lines makes it nearly impossible to track which line you're reading. Your eye constantly loses its place and has to re-find the correct line.

BAD: Extremely long lines of text

This is a wall of text that spans the entire width of the container without any max-width constraint. Research shows that optimal line length for readability is between 45 and 75 characters per line. When text extends much beyond that, the reader's eye has difficulty tracking from the end of one line to the beginning of the next line, causing fatigue and reducing comprehension. This is particularly problematic on wide screens where the container might be 800+ pixels wide, resulting in extremely long lines that are painful to read. The ideal solution is to either constrain the container width or set a max-width on the text element itself.
